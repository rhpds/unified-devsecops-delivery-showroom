= Module 2: Sign and Verify All Artifacts With *RHTAS*

== Introduction 

In the previous module, you established visibility into what is inside your software by analyzing *SBOMs*. The next challenge is ensuring that what gets built and delivered has not been altered along the way.
In modern software delivery, artifacts move across multiple systems, build pipelines, registries, and environments. Without a way to verify their origin and integrity, organizations are exposed to risks such as tampering, unauthorized changes, or compromised supply chains.

This is where *Red Hat Trusted Artifact Signer (RHTAS)* comes in. *RHTAS* provides a platform capability for signing and verifying software artifacts, allowing organizations to confirm that what they are deploying was produced by a trusted process and has not been modified.

In this module, you will see how trusted signing is embedded directly into the platform. As artifacts are built, they are automatically signed, creating a verifiable record of their origin and integrity as they move through the delivery lifecycle.

.Optional Deep Dive: How does keyless signing work?
[%collapsible]
====

You’ve now completed this module. The section below is optional and provides a deeper look at how keyless signing works behind the scenes.

At a high level, *RHTAS* uses link:https://www.sigstore.dev[*Sigstore project*^] components to provide identity-based, auditable signing:

. *Fulcio* issues short-lived signing certificates tied to a verified identity
. *Rekor* records signing events in a tamper-evident transparency log
. *Cosign* signs and verifies container images
. *TUF* manages trusted root metadata for verification

Together, these components ensure that every signature is tied to a real identity, recorded in an immutable log, and verifiable at any time.

---

*Signing flow in this workshop*

. A *Tekton Task* builds and pushes an image to the registry
. *Tekton Chains* detects the successful pipeline execution
. *Cosign* requests an identity token from the identity provider (*RHBK*)
. *Fulcio* issues a short-lived certificate for that identity
. The image is signed and the signature is recorded in *Rekor*
. The signature is stored alongside the image in the registry (*Quay*)

image::keyless_signing_flow.png[keyless_signing_flow,align="center",link=self,window=blank]

---

*Why keyless signing matters*

Traditional signing relies on long-lived private keys that must be created, stored, and rotated.

With keyless signing:
. No long-lived keys are managed by developers or pipelines  
. Every signature is tied to a verified identity  
. All signing events are recorded and auditable  

This reduces operational overhead while significantly improving trust and traceability across the software supply chain.
